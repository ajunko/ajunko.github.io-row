Definition

Rowing is the "propelling of a boat using a fixed oar as a lever" according to Olympics.com.

Ancient History

The action of rowing was a method in which individuals used to transport goods, services, and people in Egypt, Greece, and Rome.

The act of rowing was also used to propel war vessels and ships in both Egypt and in the Roman Empire.

Transportation of passengers thorugh the Thames River in London began in the 13th century.

Becoming a Sport

Entering the 19th century, rowing came to popularity in the United States in the form of ferrymen racing against one another.

On the collegiate level, universities such as Oxford and Cambridge began to pick up the sport through the formation of teams in the 1820s.

The year 1839 marked the first occurence of the Henley Regatta which still happens today.

American universities such as Harvard and Yale began racing in 1851 which jumpstarted a chain reaction for other institutions in similar standing.

Olympics and Beyond

The sport was added to the Olympics in 1900 for men and 1976 for woemen.

Currently, the sport consists of a 2,000 meter course in which crews compete in either sculling or sweeping boats.
